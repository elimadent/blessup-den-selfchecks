You have seen them grouped as "nootropic peptides," as if they were interchangeable brain-boosters: Semax for focus, Selank for calm, Cerebrolysin for recovery, Dihexa for raw synapse-building. They are not interchangeable, and they are not equally proven.

Here is the honest version. Each of these works through a genuinely different mechanism, and each sits on a different rung of the evidence ladder. The mechanisms are interesting, sometimes elegant. But the human proof is thin and uneven: strongest (though still only modest) for one of them, mostly from Russian-language studies for two, and essentially absent for the trendiest one, whose headline research paper was retracted for fabricated data in 2025. This is a class where the marketing and the mechanism have run well ahead of the evidence, so the useful thing we can do is line up the mechanism against the proof, honestly, one compound at a time. We do honest, not hype.

The basics

Four peptides, four different jobs

The thing that groups these together is loose: they are all peptides studied for the brain. But they work through genuinely different mechanisms, and the honest way to hold them apart is by mechanism and by evidence at the same time.

  • Semax is a fragment of a stress hormone (ACTH), re-tuned to raise BDNF, the brain's main growth signal, without raising cortisol. The focus-and-neuroprotection one.
  • Selank is derived from an immune peptide (tuftsin) and gently tunes the GABA calming system, without the sedation or dependence of a benzodiazepine. The calm one.
  • Cerebrolysin is not one molecule at all, it is a mixture of small peptides from pig brain that both mimics and induces the brain's own growth factors. The clinical, injectable one, with the most trials.
  • Dihexa was designed to drive synaptogenesis (new synapses) through a growth-factor pathway. Astonishing in animals, untested in humans, and its key paper was retracted.

The honest half-truth you will hear

"These are proven brain-boosters." Half true, and the halves matter. Cerebrolysin has real trials, but the results are modest and mixed. Semax and Selank have genuine human data, but it is largely Russian-language and does not meet Western trial standards. And Dihexa, the one with the most exciting story, has no human trials at all, and the paper that established its mechanism was retracted for fabricated data. Interesting mechanism is not the same as proven benefit.

The part the marketing flips

The evidence gradient is the whole story

These get sold as if they are equally legitimate cognitive tools. They are not, and the differences are large. Here is an honest read of how much real human evidence sits behind each. Longer bars mean deeper, more rigorous human data, not "feels stronger."

Under the hood

Four different ways into the brain

SEMAX

Raise the growth signal

Activates brain melanocortin receptors to push up BDNF and related growth factors and blood flow, sharpening focus, without raising cortisol the way its parent hormone would.

SELANK

Tune the calm system

Nudges the GABA calming system (without binding the sedative benzodiazepine site) and touches serotonin, easing anxiety without drowsiness, tolerance, or dependence.

CEREBROLYSIN

Feed and induce growth factors

A mixture of small peptides that both mimics the brain's growth factors and prompts the brain to make more of its own, while damping inflammation. Not one lever, a cocktail.

DIHEXA

Build new synapses (proposed)

Designed to prolong a growth-factor pathway (HGF and its c-Met receptor) that drives new synapse formation. Extremely potent in cell and animal work; the human chain is unproven, and its key paper was retracted.

The four, with honest pros and cons

Semax, Selank, Cerebrolysin, and Dihexa

Different mechanisms, different rungs of proof. Tags show the lean: focus, calm, clinical, or experimental.

Semax

Focus · BDNF

A stress-hormone fragment re-tuned to raise the brain's growth signal without the cortisol. The focus and neuroprotection one.

Pros
  • Clean, non-jittery focus and drive that many users feel within days
  • Raises BDNF and is genuinely neuroprotective in the research, without raising cortisol
  • A prescription medicine in Russia, so there is real human use behind it
Cons
  • The human data is largely Russian-language and does not meet Western trial standards
  • Stimulating, so late-day dosing disrupts sleep; overstimulation at higher doses
  • General cognitive enhancement in healthy adults is extrapolated, not directly proven

Honest read: the morning focus peptide, real but under-vetted in the West. Intranasal or SubQ, morning only. Classically paired with Selank at night.

Selank

Calm · GABA

A tuftsin-derived peptide that calms without sedating. The anxiety one, and the best-tolerated of the class.

Pros
  • Calm without sedation, cognitive fog, tolerance, or dependence
  • In a Russian trial its anxiolytic effect was comparable to a benzodiazepine, without the downsides
  • Very well tolerated, with a bonus immune-modulating angle
Cons
  • Same caveat as Semax: the human data is primarily Russian, Western trials are limited
  • It touches serotonin, so it needs caution with antidepressants and serotonergic meds
  • Injectable use is extrapolated from the approved nasal form

Honest read: the evening calm peptide, genuinely gentle. Intranasal or SubQ. Talk to a provider first if you take psychiatric medication.

Cerebrolysin

Clinical · the evidenced one

Not one molecule but a peptide mixture from pig brain, run as a course of injections. The one with real, if mixed, human trials.

Pros
  • The most clinically studied member: decades of trials in stroke, dementia, and brain injury
  • A positive meta-analysis in mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's showed a real cognitive benefit
  • A genuine, multi-target neurotrophic mechanism, approved in dozens of countries
Cons
  • The results are mixed: a 2023 Cochrane review found no benefit on survival in acute stroke, and flagged a possible increase in non-fatal serious events
  • An injectable, animal-derived biological mixture, so allergy and batch variability are real
  • Not FDA-approved; for healthy enhancement the evidence is thin and extrapolated

Honest read: promising, not proven, and a clinician's tool. Given by IM or IV in courses, for a specific neurorecovery goal, with a provider. Not a feel-it-today nootropic.

Dihexa

Experimental · wait for data

An angiotensin-derived peptide reported to be extraordinarily potent at building synapses in animals. The trendiest, and the least proven.

Pros
  • Striking potency in cell and animal models: active at tiny concentrations
  • The appeal is structural plasticity (new synapses), not a stimulant feel
  • A mechanistically interesting target worth continued research
Cons
  • No human trials at all: no human dosing, safety window, or efficacy data
  • Its foundational 2014 mechanism paper was retracted in 2025 for fabricated data, and a 2024 animal study was a flat null
  • Its pathway (c-Met) is a cancer-related growth pathway, so a growth agonist with no long-term data carries a theoretical cancer flag

Honest read: a research story, not a run-it-well compound. The sensible move is to follow the science and wait for human data, not to be the experiment.

Why the honesty matters here

When the headline claim loses its paper

Dihexa is famous online for a stunning number: reportedly far more potent than BDNF at building synapses. That claim traces back to a specific 2014 research paper. According to PubMed, that paper was retracted in 2025 for falsified and fabricated figures (retraction notice doi:10.1016/j.jpet.2025.103567). The remaining studies make the mechanism a reasonable hypothesis to investigate, not a settled fact to market. Add a 2024 animal study that found no benefit, and no human trials at all, and the honest verdict is simple.

The Dihexa verdict

There is no established safety profile, no validated dose, and no human exposure data, and the clearest mechanistic paper no longer stands. This is a compound where the enthusiasm has run far ahead of the evidence. The sensible action is to watch replication and trials from the sidelines, and build the proven neuroplasticity foundation (below) now.

The cautions that matter

The safety picture

These are generally milder than the injectable hormones elsewhere in the library, but "research chemical with limited oversight" is the honest frame for most of them, and a few lines are firm.

1

Seizure disorder or epilepsy

These are neuro-active agents, and a seizure signal has been reported with Cerebrolysin at high doses. Any personal or family history of seizures is a reason to stop and talk to a neurologist before considering any of them.

2

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

None of these has the safety data to be used in pregnancy or breastfeeding. Clear that first.

3

Dihexa is experimental, and touches a cancer pathway

No human data means no known safety window. And its growth-factor pathway (c-Met) is involved in cancer biology, so anyone with an active or recent cancer, or a hereditary cancer risk, has especially little basis for accepting that uncertainty.

4

Pork or animal-product allergy (Cerebrolysin)

Cerebrolysin is derived from pig brain, so a known allergy to porcine or biological protein products is a firm contraindication, and a supervised test dose is prudent for the first course regardless.

5

Antidepressants and serotonergic medication

Selank modulates serotonin, so if you take an SSRI or other serotonergic or psychiatric medication, that is a provider conversation before you add anything here.

The one rule under all of it

Match the compound to a real goal, keep anything clinical (Cerebrolysin above all) with a provider, be honest that Semax and Selank are under-vetted in the West, and leave Dihexa on the shelf until there is human data. And build the foundation below first, because it does more than any of these.

The move most people skip

The biggest cognitive gains in your near future come from sleep, exercise, and learning, not from a vial. Fix those first, then, if you still want to, match the mildest compound to a real goal with honest eyes on the evidence. Matching beats maxing.

The foundation under everything

What actually builds a better brain

Sleep, first

Memory is consolidated and the brain is cleared and repaired during sleep. No peptide compensates for short or broken sleep, and several of these disrupt it if dosed late. Fix sleep before you spend a dollar on a vial.

Exercise is the real BDNF driver

Aerobic exercise reliably raises BDNF, the exact growth signal Semax nudges pharmacologically. It is the most evidence-backed cognitive intervention there is, and it is free.

Learning and challenge

The brain builds connections through use: learning new skills, real cognitive challenge, and social engagement. That is genuine synaptogenesis, with far better evidence than any synapse-building peptide.

The metabolic basics

Blood-pressure and glucose control, hearing and vision correction, adequate protein and B-vitamins, and treating documented deficiencies do more for long-term cognition than any compound on this page.

Cutting through the noise

Support, graded honestly

Solid

Sleep, aerobic exercise, and real cognitive challenge

The genuinely evidence-backed way to build a better brain. This is what the peptides only nudge on top of. Boring, and it works.

Reasonable

Omega-3s, creatine, and correcting documented deficiencies

Sensible support with reasonable evidence, especially B12, folate, iron, vitamin D, and thyroid if a lab says you are low. Follow the labs, not the hype.

Unproven

Stacking several "nootropic peptides" at once for a bigger effect

Each added compound is another layer of unknown interaction on top of already-thin data. More is not more here; it is just more uncertainty.

Skip

Dihexa as a synapse-builder, on the strength of the internet claim

No human data, a retracted foundational paper, and a cancer-pathway flag. This is the one to leave on the shelf until real human evidence exists.

Stop and get medical help

A few situations are not "go slower," they are "stop and be seen."

  • Any seizure, convulsion, or fainting spell
  • Signs of a severe allergic reaction: swelling of the face or throat, trouble breathing
  • Severe agitation, confusion, or a sudden change in mental state
  • Severe or persistent headache that is new for you
  • Any symptom that feels like a serotonin reaction if you take antidepressants (agitation, fast heartbeat, sweating, tremor)

Mild nasal irritation or a short headache settles. A seizure, a breathing reaction, or a sudden mental-state change do not wait. When in doubt, be seen.

The BlessUp take

Line the mechanism up against the proof

This is a class where the ideas are genuinely interesting and the evidence is genuinely uneven, and the honest move is to hold both at once. Semax raises the brain's growth signal and Selank calms it, both real medicines in Russia, both under-vetted in the West. Cerebrolysin is the one with real trials, and even it is only modest and mixed, promising in dementia, no survival benefit in stroke. And Dihexa is the cautionary tale of the whole space: a thrilling story, no human data, and a foundational paper retracted for fabrication. So do not buy the mechanism as if it were the outcome. Fix your sleep, your exercise, and your learning first, because those out-perform everything on this page. Then, if you still want to, match the mildest compound to a real goal, keep anything clinical with a provider, and leave the unproven one on the shelf until the science catches up. That is not the timid move. It is the one that respects your brain.
