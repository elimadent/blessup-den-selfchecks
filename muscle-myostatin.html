Myostatin blockade reliably adds muscle mass, but human trials repeatedly failed to turn that into strength, and the one in-class human program (ACE-031) was stopped over bleeding side effects. The growth-factor members have no human muscle trials at all. Full citations at the end.

You have seen them sold as the cheat code: Follistatin, ACE-031, IGF-1 LR3, and MGF, peptides that promise to override your body's limits and pack on muscle faster than training alone ever could.

Here is the honest version. These four split into two completely different strategies. Follistatin and ACE-031 are myostatin inhibitors: they release a natural brake. Your body makes a protein called myostatin whose whole job is to LIMIT how much muscle you build, and these mop it up so the brake comes off. IGF-1 LR3 and MGF work the opposite way, they are growth factors that press the accelerator, directly signaling muscle to grow. Both sound great on paper. But the proof is not there. The only member ever tested in real human trials, ACE-031, was halted for safety. The growth factors have never been trialed for muscle in people and carry a genuine cancer caution, because a signal that tells cells to grow does not only talk to muscle. So the useful thing we can do is line each one up against what it actually does and the proof it actually has. We do honest, not hype.

The basics

Two strategies, one goal

Your body actively limits its own muscle. A protein called myostatin is the brake: animals born without it (and a handful of rare humans) grow enormous, dense muscle. That single fact is the seed of this whole class. Two of these peptides release that brake; the other two skip it and push a growth signal directly:

  • Follistatin (FST-344). A natural protein that binds and neutralizes myostatin (and related "activins"), taking the brake off. The dramatic muscle results come from gene therapy, not the injectable version sold online.
  • ACE-031. An engineered decoy receptor that soaks up myostatin before it can act, a broader, longer-lasting brake release. The only member with real human trials, and they were halted for safety.
  • IGF-1 LR3. A long-acting version of a natural growth factor (IGF-1) that presses the accelerator directly. Powerful on paper, unproven in people, and the highest-caution member.
  • MGF (IGF-1Ec). A local "mechano-growth" splice of IGF-1 your muscle releases in a brief pulse after hard training, studied for local repair. Almost no human trial data for the injected form.

The honest half-truth you will hear

"They let you break past your genetic limit." Two problems. First, releasing the myostatin brake reliably adds muscle SIZE, but human trials repeatedly failed to turn that size into real STRENGTH or function, and your genetics still set a ceiling. Second, the growth-factor members do not have human muscle trials at all, and "tell every cell to grow" is exactly why the cancer caution is real. Impressive mechanism, thin and cautionary proof.

Under the hood

Release the brake, or press the accelerator

01

The brake (myostatin)

Follistatin and ACE-031 mop up myostatin, the protein your body uses to cap muscle growth. Take the brake off and the muscle-building pathway runs more freely. You are removing a limit, not adding fuel.

02

The accelerator (IGF-1)

IGF-1 LR3 and MGF are growth signals. They switch on muscle protein synthesis and wake up satellite cells (the repair crew). This is pressing the gas directly, which is more potent and less selective.

03

Why the difference matters

A brake release has a ceiling (your genes still set the limit) and touches other tissues, ACE-031's off-target effect on blood vessels is what halted its trial. An accelerator that says "grow" to every cell is exactly why IGF-1 LR3 and MGF carry a cancer caution. Neither route is free muscle.

That is the class in one idea: two very different ways to push on muscle, each with a real, mechanism-based catch. Understanding which lever a compound pulls tells you what it can do and, more importantly, where its risk lives.

The four, with honest pros and cons

Each one, against its evidence

Same goal, very different levels of proof. Tags show the standing: clinical, gene-therapy, or preclinical.

ACE-031

Clinical · the evidenced one

An engineered decoy receptor that soaks up myostatin and related growth-limiters. The only member with real human trials, and the only one whose safety problem we can actually see.

Pros
  • The deepest evidence of the class: real human trials in muscular dystrophy and in healthy adults
  • Produced measurable gains in lean mass and bone density in early trials
  • A broad, long-lasting brake release, mechanistically the most complete of the inhibitors
Cons
  • Its Duchenne trial was stopped early over bleeding side effects (nosebleeds, spider veins, gum bleeding) from off-target effects on blood vessels
  • The lean-mass gains did not translate into a clear strength or walking benefit
  • Development was discontinued; it is not available as an approved medicine for any muscle use

Honest read: the most-studied member, and its own trials are the clearest warning in this class: mass went up, function did not, and safety stopped it. Given by injection.

Follistatin (FST-344)

Gene-therapy · the split story

A natural myostatin neutralizer. The eye-popping muscle results come from gene therapy delivered into muscle, which is a very different thing from the injectable peptide sold online.

Pros
  • A genuinely elegant mechanism: it blocks myostatin and the activins with high potency
  • Gene-therapy trials in muscular dystrophy showed real walking-distance gains and healthier muscle tissue
  • The strongest "brake off" biology of the natural inhibitors
Cons
  • Those results are gene therapy (a one-time genetic delivery), not the repeat-injection peptide, which has essentially no human trials
  • By blocking activins it also lowers reproductive hormones (FSH), a real, mechanism-based side effect
  • Long-term effects on the heart (which myostatin also regulates) are unknown

Honest read: the mechanism is a ten; the injectable people actually buy is an open question with almost no human data. Do not confuse the gene therapy for the vial. Given by injection.

IGF-1 LR3

Preclinical · the highest caution

A long-acting, engineered version of the growth factor IGF-1 that presses the accelerator directly. The most potent-sounding and, honestly, the riskiest of the four.

Pros
  • A direct, powerful anabolic signal in animal and cell studies
  • Engineered to stay active far longer than natural IGF-1
  • Its natural cousin is an approved medicine for a rare deficiency, so the biology is real
Cons
  • No human trials for building muscle, and much of the fast "gain" is water and glycogen, not lasting tissue
  • A real, mechanism-based cancer concern: it is a growth signal to every cell, not just muscle
  • Can drop blood sugar dangerously (it cross-reacts with insulin), with a long tail that makes a low linger

Honest read: loud on paper, unproven and high-risk in practice. The cancer and low-blood-sugar cautions are the headline, not the muscle. Given by injection.

MGF (IGF-1Ec)

Preclinical · the local repair signal

A local "mechano-growth" splice of IGF-1 that muscle releases in a brief pulse after hard training, studied for kick-starting repair. Interesting biology, barely any human product data.

Pros
  • A real, well-described natural signal: training genuinely raises MGF in muscle
  • Wakes up satellite cells, the repair crew, in animal models
  • Aimed locally at the muscle worked, in theory a targeted recovery nudge
Cons
  • The injected peptide has essentially no controlled human trials, and native MGF lasts only minutes in the body
  • Most reported effects are hard to separate from the training that would happen anyway
  • Being an IGF-family growth signal, it carries the same cancer caution, more so in the longer-lasting PEG version

Honest read: a small local nudge on top of training at best, and unproven in people. The training itself is doing most of the work. Given by local injection.

The line that matters most

Bigger is not the same as stronger

The single most important idea in this class is the gap between size and function. Releasing the myostatin brake reliably makes muscles bigger, that part is real. But when researchers measured whether that extra size made people actually stronger or more mobile, the benefit kept failing to show up. A larger muscle that is not proportionally stronger is a cosmetic result with real risk attached, not a performance breakthrough. And that is the member with the BEST data. The growth factors skip human testing entirely.

Two things to hold onto

First, the only in-class human program (ACE-031) was stopped for safety, not for lack of muscle effect, that tells you the risk is real and specific. Second, the two accelerator peptides (IGF-1 LR3, MGF) are growth signals, so a cancer history is a genuine stop, not a footnote. This is a class where the mechanism is genuinely exciting and the honest human evidence is genuinely thin. Hold both truths at once.

The most important section here

The hard stops and cautions

Each of these carries a specific, mechanism-based risk. A few situations are genuinely off-limits or need a provider.

1

Active or recent cancer

This is the sharpest line, and it is strongest for the growth factors, IGF-1 LR3 and MGF. They are signals that tell cells to grow and resist dying, and that is precisely the wrong message anywhere a malignancy exists or might. This is a mechanistic concern rather than a documented event, but it is a firm reason to stop and involve an oncologist, never to self-experiment.

2

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

None of these has human safety data in pregnancy or breastfeeding, and Follistatin also lowers reproductive hormones (FSH), which matters if you are trying to conceive. Clear all of this first.

3

IGF-1 LR3 and blood sugar

IGF-1 LR3 cross-reacts with the insulin receptor and can drop blood sugar sharply, with a long tail that makes a low linger for hours. Never inject it fasted, never stack it with insulin or glucose-lowering drugs, and keep fast-acting sugar on hand. Diabetes makes this a provider-only conversation.

4

ACE-031 and bleeding

The exact thing that halted ACE-031's trial was off-target effects on blood vessels: nosebleeds, spider veins, and gum bleeding. A bleeding disorder or a blood thinner turns that from a nuisance into a real risk. This is a specific, documented caution, not a theoretical one.

5

Research status and competition testing

None of these is approved for building muscle, purity and sourcing are unregulated, and long-term safety is unknown. All are banned by the World Anti-Doping Agency (growth factors and gene doping), so they are strictly off-limits for any drug-tested athlete.

The one rule under all of it

If you have any cancer history, the growth factors here are not a self-directed project, clear it with an oncologist first. Otherwise, be honest that the human proof is thin, respect the specific risk each one carries, and build the foundation below first, because for actual muscle it does far more than any of these.

The foundation under everything

What actually builds muscle

Here is the part no muscle peptide wants you to hear: the things that reliably build muscle are free, proven, and boring, and they dwarf everything on this list. These peptides, at best, nudge on top of a foundation. Without it, there is nothing to nudge.

Progressive resistance training

Lifting with steadily increasing challenge is the actual signal that builds muscle. Nothing here replaces it; the growth factors are literally trying to imitate a fraction of what a hard set already does. Train hard and consistently first.

Protein and enough calories

Muscle is built from protein, and you cannot grow in a deep deficit. Roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of bodyweight, plus enough total food, does more for muscle than any compound in this class. This is the real lever.

Sleep and recovery

Muscle is built during recovery, not during the workout. Short or broken sleep blunts growth and strength directly. No peptide compensates for chronic under-recovery. Protect it.

Patience and honest genetics

Real muscle takes years, and your genetics set a ceiling that no brake-release fully overrides. Chasing a shortcut usually trades a small, unproven bump for a real risk. The slow way is the one that actually works.

Stop and get medical help

A few situations are not "go slower," they are "stop and be seen."

  • Symptoms of low blood sugar (shaking, sweating, confusion), especially on IGF-1 LR3
  • Unusual bleeding: frequent nosebleeds, bleeding gums, or new spider veins
  • A severe allergic reaction: swelling of the face or throat, trouble breathing
  • A spreading, hot, or painful injection site (a possible infection)
  • Any new or unusual lump or symptom you are worried about

A mild injection-site reaction settles. A low-blood-sugar episode, unusual bleeding, or an allergic reaction do not wait. When in doubt, be seen.

The BlessUp take

Respect the mechanism, doubt the shortcut

The idea here is genuinely seductive: your body caps its own muscle with a brake called myostatin, and these peptides either release that brake or press the accelerator directly. The biology is real and, in the lab, dramatic. But the honest human story is a long way behind the hype. Releasing the brake reliably adds SIZE, and then repeatedly fails to add real STRENGTH. The one member ever tested in people, ACE-031, was stopped over bleeding side effects. And the two growth factors that sound the most powerful, IGF-1 LR3 and MGF, have never been trialed for muscle in humans and carry a real cancer caution, because a signal that shouts "grow" does not only reach your biceps. So do not confuse an exciting mechanism for a proven result, and do not confuse a gene-therapy headline for the vial in your fridge. For actual muscle, the boring foundation, hard progressive training, enough protein, real sleep, and years of patience, out-builds every one of these, at zero risk. If you still have a real reason to explore this class, go in knowing the proof is thin, respect the specific danger each one carries, and do it with a provider. That is not the timid move. It is the one that respects both how the body grows and how little we have actually proven.
