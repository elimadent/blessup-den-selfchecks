You have seen them sold as three separate things: Melanotan I and II for a tan, PT-141 for libido. In truth they are close cousins, all synthetic versions of the same natural hormone (alpha-MSH), all working the same small family of receptors.

Here is the honest version. Your body has a set of melanocortin receptors, and two of them matter here. One, MC1R, sits on your skin's pigment cells and drives tanning. The other, MC4R, sits in your brain and drives sexual arousal and appetite. Each of these three compounds simply hits those receptors in a different mix: one leans toward the tan, one hits everything at once, and one is tuned for the libido effect with the tan dialed down. That single idea explains the whole class. And because two of them stimulate pigment cells, the most important part of this entire report is not the tan or the libido, it is your moles. We do honest, not hype, so let us take it slowly.

The basics

One hormone, two receptors

All three are lab-made versions of alpha-MSH, a natural hormone that talks to your melanocortin receptors. Two of those receptors do the work you hear about:

  • MC1R lives on the pigment cells in your skin. Switch it on and those cells make more melanin, the brown pigment, so your skin darkens. That is the tan.
  • MC4R lives in your brain, in the circuits for sexual arousal and appetite. Switch it on and you get more desire and arousal, and less hunger. That is the libido (and the appetite-drop) effect.

Every difference between these three compounds comes down to which of those two receptors it favors:

The honest half-truth you will hear

"They are just tanning peptides, basically harmless." Half true. The tan is real and the mechanism is well understood, but two of these broadly stimulate the pigment cells that can turn into melanoma, they darken existing moles, and the long-term safety of that has never been established in a trial. Melanotan II in particular is not approved anywhere and comes from an unregulated supply. Natural-sounding is not the same as safe, and the mole risk is the real story.

The part the marketing flips

The evidence ladder, honestly

These get sold as if they are equally legitimate. They are not. Two have real regulatory approval and trial data (for specific medical uses); one has neither and rests on gray-market experience. Longer bars mean deeper, real human evidence, not "works better for a tan."

Under the hood

How one hormone does three things

01

Bind the receptor

Each compound is an alpha-MSH analog. Melanotan I leans on MC1R; Melanotan II hits the whole family; PT-141 favors the brain's MC4R.

02

MC1R makes the pigment

On skin cells, MC1R activation switches on melanin production. Fresh pigment builds over weeks, so the tan is cumulative and fades slowly, and it stimulates every pigment cell, including your moles.

03

MC4R works the brain

In the brain, MC4R activation raises sexual desire and arousal (upstream of the plumbing, unlike the usual ED pills) and lowers appetite. The same brain region also drives the nausea.

That last point matters: the nausea so many people feel is not a contaminant or a bad batch, it is the MC4R effect itself, coming from the same brain circuitry that produces the benefit. It usually settles over the first several doses, which is why starting low exists.

The three, with honest pros and cons

Melanotan I, Melanotan II, and PT-141

Same hormone family, different receptor mix. Tags show the lean: tan, tan and libido, or libido.

Melanotan I (afamelanotide)

Tan · MC1R

The more MC1R-selective one, and the version with an actual approval. The cleaner tanning member.

Pros
  • More selective for pigment, so fewer of the central effects (little nausea, no libido or appetite change)
  • The approved member: real trial data and long-term safety monitoring behind the medical version
  • A longer-lasting, more even tan
Cons
  • Slower to show, and it still needs some UV to develop a visible tan
  • Its approval is for a rare light disorder, not for cosmetic tanning; off-label tanning use is unstudied
  • Still stimulates pigment cells, so moles and freckles can darken

Honest read: the cleaner tan for someone whose only goal is pigment. Still requires the mole discipline below. Approved medically as an implant; off-label tanning use is by injection.

Melanotan II

Tan + libido · broad

The non-selective one: it hits the whole melanocortin family at once, so it does everything, and brings the most side effects.

Pros
  • The strongest, fastest tan, and it works largely without UV
  • A real libido and erectile effect (in a small study, erections in 17 of 20 men), plus appetite suppression
  • Potent at tiny doses, so a vial lasts
Cons
  • The most side effects: nausea and facial flushing are common, plus the strongest mole-darkening burden
  • Not approved anywhere; gray-market supply with no quality oversight
  • Carries a real priapism (prolonged, painful erection) risk and transient blood-pressure rises

Honest read: the do-everything one, with the heaviest safety load. Only for someone who accepts the flags and does the mole discipline. Start at the lowest dose to gauge nausea.

PT-141 (bremelanotide)

Libido · MC4R

Derived from Melanotan II but re-tuned toward the brain's MC4R, with the tanning minimized. The libido one, with an approval behind it.

Pros
  • Approved (as Vyleesi) for low sexual desire in premenopausal women, with Phase 3 trial data
  • Works on desire itself, in the brain, which the usual blood-flow ED pills do not reach
  • Taken as needed, and it clears the same day; minimal tanning
Cons
  • Nausea is common (around 40% in trials), plus flushing and headache
  • Causes transient blood-pressure changes and is contraindicated in uncontrolled high blood pressure or heart disease
  • Some skin darkening can still occur with repeated use; limited to a set number of doses per month

Honest read: the evidence-backed, tanning-minimized libido option, but not for uncontrolled blood pressure. As-needed, about 45 minutes before; not for uncontrolled hypertension or known heart disease.

The most important section here

The skin rules they all share

This applies to every compound here, because every one of them stimulates the pigment cells that can become melanoma. Get this wrong and the risk is not a side effect, it is a missed cancer. This is the discipline that makes the difference.

1

Get a baseline skin check FIRST

Before your first dose, see a dermatologist for a full skin and mole check, and photograph your moles. You cannot tell whether a mole changed later if you never mapped it at the start. This is the single most important step.

2

Melanoma or atypical-mole history is a hard stop

A personal or family history of melanoma, atypical (dysplastic) moles, or a large number of moles is a firm contraindication, not a caution. Stimulating pigment cells is exactly the wrong move for that skin, full stop.

3

Watch every mole by the ABCDE rule

For the whole time you use it: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color change, Diameter growth, Evolving. Any new, growing, or asymmetrically changing mole is a stop-and-see-a-dermatologist signal, not a wait-and-see one.

4

A tanning peptide is not sunscreen

A darker tan gives only a little UV protection and does not replace sun protection. Keep wearing sunscreen. Chasing a tan while skipping SPF, and stimulating pigment cells at the same time, is working against yourself twice.

5

Start low, for the nausea

Nausea and flushing are the common early effects (worst with Melanotan II and PT-141). Start at the lowest dose, dose in the evening, and consider a small meal or an anti-nausea aid beforehand. It usually eases within a few doses.

The Melanotan II extra

Melanotan II carries a specific priapism risk: an erection that will not go down. An erection lasting more than four hours is a medical emergency, not something to wait out, because it can permanently damage tissue. Know that going in, especially with unregulated product at uncertain doses.

Put it together

How to think about choosing, honestly

First question

Are you cleared at all?

  • No melanoma or skin-cancer history, no atypical moles or heavy mole count
  • A baseline dermatology skin check done and moles photographed
  • Not pregnant or breastfeeding; blood pressure controlled (especially for PT-141)
  • If a skin hard stop is present, this class is not the tool, full stop

Second question

Which one fits the goal?

  • Tan, cleaner profile: Melanotan I
  • Tan plus libido, eyes open on side effects: Melanotan II
  • Libido only, with an approval behind it: PT-141
  • Never same-day-stack two of these: they hit overlapping receptors

The move most people skip

The dermatology baseline is not optional garnish, it is the whole safety story. Do that first, match the compound to the actual goal, start low, and do not combine two melanocortins. Matching beats maxing.

The foundation under everything

The habits that make this safe

Mole-mapping and an annual skin check

Photograph your moles before you start, watch them by the ABCDE rule throughout, and keep an annual dermatology check. This is the single highest-leverage habit on this entire class, and it is free.

Sun protection, still

The tan is not armor. Keep using sunscreen, especially since you are actively stimulating pigment cells. Sensible sun exposure, not more of it.

Nausea management

Start low, dose in the evening, eat a little first, and give it a few doses to settle. Most people adapt within a week; if you never do, that compound may not be for you.

Blood-pressure awareness for PT-141

PT-141 nudges blood pressure transiently. Know your numbers, and do not use it if your blood pressure is uncontrolled or you have known heart disease.

The checks worth knowing

  • A full-body skin and mole map: the first and most important screen for this whole class.
  • Blood pressure: especially before and on PT-141.
  • Personal and family melanoma history: can take the class off the table entirely.

Stop and get medical help

A few situations are not "go slower," they are "stop and be seen."

  • Any new, growing, or changing mole (Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolving)
  • An erection lasting more than four hours (a medical emergency)
  • Severe or persistent headache, chest pain, or signs of very high blood pressure
  • A mole that bleeds, itches, or will not heal
  • Severe allergic reaction: swelling of the face or throat, trouble breathing

A little nausea or flushing settles. A changing mole or a four-hour erection do not wait, they are emergencies. When in doubt about your skin, see a dermatologist.
